Locard’s Exchange Principle and the Case That Got It Wrong: Kristine Bunch (1995)

I started this post thinking I’d talk about Locard’s Principle and like, fiber evidence or maybe a hair left on a doorknob. Instead, I looked into a case where the principle was technically used correctly, but the science? The interpretation? The entire forensic thing? All wrong. WRONG. ahem—

Kristine Bunch was wrongfully convicted of arson and murder because investigators thought they found proof she set her trailer on fire. What they actually found was: a kerosene heater. And a ton of bad science built on misread burn marks and outdated assumptions.

What Is Locard’s Exchange Principle?

This is Forensics 101. Edmond Locard said:

Every contact leaves a trace.

If you touch something, you leave something behind. And if you enter a space, that space leaves something on you. Hair, fibers, soil, skin cells, oil, cat hair, whatever—you bring evidence with you, and you take it with you when you go.

In theory, that’s how you catch people. It’s a beautiful concept, truly (maybe Locard can thank me from his grave.) But if you misuse the traces—like if you don’t question what was transferred, how it got there, or what it actually means—you can build an entire case on a lie.

The Kristine Bunch Case (1995)

Hope, Indiana. June 30th, 1995.

Kristine Bunch, 22, was home with her 3-year-old son, Tony, when her trailer went up in flames.

Kristine survived the fire. Tony didn’t. And within hours, she was treated as a suspect.

Why did the investigators say she did it?

“Multiple Points of Origin”

Firefighters said the burn damage in the living room and south bedroom looked like it came from two separate fires. In their minds, this meant the fires were set intentionally and separately, not from one accidental ignition.

“Pour Patterns” on the Floor

Investigators reported strange burn marks across the floor that LOOKED like someone had dumped gasoline or kerosene and lit it. These shapes were interpreted as “pour patterns.”

Presence of Accelerants

An ATF chemist claimed lab tests found petroleum distillates in samples from both rooms. This matched the “intentional arson” theory.

These three things: multiple origins, pour patterns, and accelerant detection, were the whole case. It led to a 60-year sentence.

The Science (And Where It Failed)

One Fire, Not Two

The fire started near a kerosene heater in the living room. Because trailers are highly flammable, it spread quickly, therefore creating the illusion of a second fire.

Pour Patterns Are Not Always Pour Patterns

The irregular burns were caused by flashover and synthetic materials—not accelerants. Old-school arson beliefs were just... wrong.

No Accelerants in the Bedroom

New analysis found no accelerants in the bedroom at all. The living room? Kerosene. From her space heater. That was... known. And ignored.

This wasn't just bad science—it was buried science. The very evidence that could’ve cleared her was used to convict her.

So... What About Locard?

Locard's Principle was followed: they found trace chemicals. But the interpretation was wrong.

“We found a flammable substance. That must mean she poured it.”
—Investigator logic, 1995

The reality was that she lived there. The kerosene was from her heater. The “traces” weren’t criminal, they were environmental.

Context matters. A trace isn’t guilt. It’s a clue. And if you don’t interpret it right, you destroy lives.

What I Learned

I came here for fiber analysis and “aha!” moments.

Instead, I learned this: a trace is potential, not proof. Interpretation is everything.

Final Words

Locard’s Exchange Principle is iconic. But if you use it wrong, it stops being science and starts being a weapon.

Every contact leaves a trace. But not every trace tells the truth.

Sometimes it just tells you what you want to hear.
